galaxy formation / evolution
Galaxies in observations and state-of-the-art simulations broadly fall into two categories: star-forming and quiescent [1809.01665]. Over cosmic time, significant fractions of star-forming galaxies rapidly ``quench'' their star formation and become quiescent. In [1412.7162], I investigated the role that environment plays in this phenomenon using PRIMUS, a spectroscopic survey to z=1 with 120,000 galaxy redshifts.

Hierarchical structure formation predicted by ΛCDM provides a concrete framework for galaxy formation and evolution: central galaxies reside at the core of dark matter halos while satellite galaxies orbit around them. Exploiting this framework, I used empirical models that combine cosmological N-body simulations with observed trends in the galaxy population (e.g. stellar mass function) to measure the timescale of star formation quenching in central galaxies [1609.04398], debunk "galactic conformity" [1702.01121], and measure signatures of "assembly bias" [1705.08458, 1610.01991].

large-scale structure (LSS)
BOSS, eBOSS, and in the future DESI will map the spatial distribution of galaxies over an unprecedented cosmic volume in order to probe fundamental physics. Baryon Acoustic Oscillations observed in galaxy clustering can be used to measure the expansion rate of the universe, and thus constrain dark energy. Meanwhile, redshift-space distortions (RSD) in galaxy clustering can be used to measure the growth rate of structure and test General Relatively (GR).

Fiber-fed spectrographs of these surveys are critical for collecting millions of redshifts; however, instrumental constraints limit fibers from being placed close to each other on the focal plane, bias galaxy clustering, and prevent accurate constraints on cosmological parameters. In [1609.01714], I developed methods that successfully accounted for this effect and extend analyses down to smaller scales than ever before. I have also contributed to measuring galaxy clustering [1607.05383] and constructing cosmological simulations for estimating the covariance matrices of BOSS and eBOSS analyses [1509.06404, 1612.06918].

Extending galaxy clustering analyses to smaller scales allows access to extra constraining power but requires reliably modeling those scales. Standard models based on perturbation theory break down on small scales due to non-linear structure formation. Models that instead use a suite of simulations spanning parameter space and interpolation schemes can more accurately emulate galaxy clustering. Emulators so far have been limited to two-point clustering statistics, which alone are severely limited by degeneracies with bias parameters. Recently, I've been developing an emulator of two-point and three-point clusterin to break these degeneracies, test GR, and constrain the total mass of neutrinos.

statistics / inference
Standard analyses make a major assumption in their parameter inference that the likelihood function is Gaussian. In LSS, even if the underlying density field is Gaussian, this assumption is incorrect. In [1803.06348], I demonstrated using non-parametric divergence estimators from machine learning that the likelihood of the BOSS RSD analysis is non-Gaussian and assuming that it's Gaussian significantly biases parameter constraints. I also developed methods for more accurately estimating the true likelihood using novel statistical techniques (e.g. independent component analysis).

Likelihood-free inference, such as Approximate Bayesian Computation, also enables accurate parameter inference without making assumptions about the likelihood. While ABC has been used in astronomy, [1607.01782] was the first to implement it in LSS and demonstrate its feasibility. It also illustrated how ABC, by relaxing assumptions on the likelihood, allows observables with intractable likelihoods to be robustly included in analyses.

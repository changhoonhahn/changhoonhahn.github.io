Galaxy Formation / Evolution


Galaxies broadly fall into two categories: star-forming and quiescent. Over cosmic time significant fractions of star-forming galaxies rapidly "quench" their star formation and become quiescent [1412.7162]. State-of-the-art galaxy formation models (hydro sims, semi-analytic models, and empirical models) qualitatively reproduce global distributions and scaling relations of galaxies in observations. However, qualitative comparisons to a mixed bag of observations are insufficient for answering key questions in galaxy formation.

As part of the IQ collaboratory, I am leading an effort to forward model observables (photometry and spectra) of simulated galaxies and compare them to observations in order to shed light on star formation quenching in galaxies. As the first step, I compared the relation between star formation rate and stellar mass of star forming galaxies in multiple galaxy formation models to reveal nearly an order of magnitude differences among them [1809.01665]. Later, we found that hydrodynamical simulations cannot reproduce the absence of quiescent isolated low mass galaxies in observations [2010.01132]. I am currently developing an empirical framework for dust attenuation in galaxies that will be incorporated into the forward model and will allow us to marginalize over dust when comparing the simulations to observations.

Hierarchical structure formation predicted by ΛCDM provides a concrete framework for galaxy formation and evolution: central galaxies reside at the core of dark matter halos while satellite galaxies orbit around them. Empirical models can be constructed in this framework by combining cosmological N-body simulations with observed trends in the galaxy population (e.g. stellar mass function) and used to constrain specific well-posed questions in galaxy formation. Using this approach, I've constrained the timescale of star formation quenching in central galaxies [1609.04398], demonstrate that large-scale "galactic conformity" is driven by contamination [1702.01121], and measure the strength of assembly bias [1705.08458, 1610.01991]. Most recently, I used empirical models to demonstrate that the stellar-to-halo mass relation can be used to constrain the timescale of star formation variability [1910.01644].
